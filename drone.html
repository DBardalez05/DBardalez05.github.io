Basketball Drone Vision

Built a YOLOv8-based computer vision pipeline for basketball shot detection. The current system detects the ball and rim, tracks shot attempts, and counts made baskets from video as the perception foundation for a future drone-based tracking system.

Overview

This project explores how computer vision can support a basketball-tracking drone system. My current focus has been building the perception side first: detecting the basketball and rim from video, determining whether a shot is made, and displaying that result through an on-screen overlay and shot counter.

Rather than treating the project as only a machine-learning model, I approached it as a larger robotics subsystem. That meant not just training YOLOv8, but also writing the logic needed to interpret imperfect detections over time, handle temporary ball loss, and turn frame-by-frame outputs into a usable event signal for shot tracking.

The broader plan is to pair this perception pipeline with a physical basketball-tracking drone. I am building toward that in stages by first validating the vision system, then testing on an off-the-shelf drone/frame platform before eventually moving toward more custom hardware.

My Contributions

  • Collected and labeled more than 1,000 video frames from basketball footage to train a custom YOLOv8 detector for the ball and rim.
  • Built the Python inference pipeline using Ultralytics YOLO and OpenCV for detection, visualization, and shot-count overlay.
  • Designed custom shot-detection logic that combined object detections with tracking and temporal state logic to determine made baskets.
  • Implemented simple ball tracking and short-horizon prediction to maintain shot detection when the ball temporarily disappeared near the rim.
  • Structured the project as a staged robotics build, with the current focus on perception and later integration planned around an initial stock-frame drone platform.

Technical Highlights

A major part of the work involved creating a usable custom dataset from basketball footage and training a YOLOv8 model to detect the two key classes in the system: the basketball and the rim. Rim detection is currently strong, while ball detection is harder because the ball is smaller, faster, and more likely to blur or disappear during critical parts of the shot.

I also built the inference pipeline in Python using Ultralytics and OpenCV. Beyond displaying bounding boxes, the system includes a live UI overlay that tracks shot makes and displays the current count during playback. That made the project more than a detection demo; it became an event-driven vision system.

To determine whether a shot was made, I wrote custom logic on top of the detector rather than relying on detections alone. The pipeline tracks the ball over time, predicts its position briefly when detections drop out, and uses a finite-state shot-detection process with multiple spatial gates around the rim to decide whether the ball has passed through the hoop. This was especially important because the ball often becomes briefly undetectable while interacting with the rim or net.

I trained an initial YOLOv8 model to detect the basketball and rim, and the current version performs well enough to validate the overall perception approach. One of the next steps is retraining on broader cases, since the model appears somewhat overfit to the data and testing conditions used so far. My machine learning coursework is also helping shape how I approach that next round of training, evaluation, and improvement.

Challenges

The hardest part of the project has been turning raw detections into reliable shot events. Detecting the rim is relatively stable, but the ball is much harder because it is small, moves quickly, and can disappear temporarily when it overlaps with the rim, backboard, or net. That means a good detector alone is not enough; the system also needs logic that can tolerate short gaps and still interpret the shot correctly.

Another challenge has been building the project in the right order. Before designing more custom drone hardware, I first need to validate that the perception pipeline is strong enough to justify tighter integration. That is why the current phase focuses on model performance, retraining, and staged testing before moving toward a more custom flight platform.
