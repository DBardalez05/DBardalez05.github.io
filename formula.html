Overview

As part of Olin Electric Motorsports, I designed a precharge/discharge controller PCB for the car’s high-voltage accumulator system. Its role is to manage how the tractive system connects to the inverter during startup by charging the DC-link capacitor in a controlled way before the main connection is completed.

This was a safety-critical board shaped by system-level constraints rather than only circuit functionality. The design had to coordinate relay control, threshold-based state transitions, and safe default behavior while fitting into the broader Formula SAE EV architecture and its HV/LV isolation requirements.

Technical Highlights

The board controls a two-stage precharge process rather than switching the tractive system on directly. In the first stage, the system charges the inverter DC-link capacitor through a resistor path to limit inrush current. Once a threshold is reached, the second stage takes over so the system can transition toward normal operation in a controlled and repeatable way.

A major part of the design involved safety and state-management logic. I worked on threshold detection and latch behavior so that “precharge done” could be captured and remembered during the active precharge window, while still guaranteeing a clean reset when precharge is inactive. That deterministic reset behavior was important because the board needed predictable startup behavior rather than relying on undefined latch state at boot.

I also designed around safe defaults and sequencing behavior at the logic level. This included ensuring that missing or floating command conditions did not produce unsafe behavior, and shaping the control logic so relay transitions behaved in a controlled way during enable and shutdown events.

Beyond schematic design, the project required careful PCB layout for a mixed HV/LV system. I had to work within the physical size imposed by the relay footprints while maintaining isolation discipline and organizing the board so the high-voltage and low-voltage sections remained clearly separated and practical to manufacture.

Design Constraints and Challenges

One of the hardest parts of the project was PCB layout. The relays imposed strong size and placement constraints, which made routing and board organization much harder than a typical low-voltage board. At the same time, the layout had to preserve HV/LV separation and reflect the isolation requirements expected in a Formula SAE EV system.

Another major challenge was getting the latch logic right. I had to repeatedly revisit the logic to account for edge cases and make sure the SR latch always had a guaranteed reset condition. That work pushed the design beyond a simple functional schematic and into failure-mode thinking, where deterministic behavior during startup and shutdown mattered just as much as nominal operation.
